The Principle

A telephone is useless if you're the only one who has one. With two people, there's one possible conversation. With five people, there are ten. With a hundred, there are nearly five thousand.

Metcalfe's Law says the value of a network is proportional to n(n-1)/2 ≈ n² — the number of possible connections between n participants. This means each new member adds value not just for themselves, but for everyone already in the network.

Reed's Law goes further: if subgroups can form (group chats, communities, DAOs), the number of possible groups is 2ⁿ, which grows even faster. This is why platforms that enable group formation (WhatsApp, Discord) can grow explosively.

The practical consequence: networks have tipping points. Below a critical mass, the value per member is low and growth is slow. Above it, every addition accelerates growth. This is why early networks often need subsidies to reach escape velocity — and why mature networks are so hard to displace.

Why This Matters

Network effects explain why the internet tends toward concentration — why there's one dominant search engine, one dominant social network, one dominant marketplace. The value curve rewards scale nonlinearly.

But they also explain why protocols can be more powerful than platforms. A protocol (email, HTTP, Bitcoin) can't be owned, which means its network effects benefit everyone rather than accruing to a single company. The question for any new network: who captures the value that network effects create?
